STA 9750 Final Project – Happiness, Income, Vacation, and Governance

1. Motivation

This report examines how national happiness relates to economic security (income) and time-related well-being (vacation/leisure), and adds an institutional lens via perceptions of corruption. The purpose is to compare which factors appear most strongly associated with happiness across countries.

2. Data Sources

  • World Happiness Report (WHR 2025 data file): happiness (life evaluation, 3-year average), explained log GDP per capita, explained perceptions of corruption.
  • OECD Time Use: Leisure minutes/day (LEI) as a work–life balance / time-off proxy.

3. Visualizations

3.1 Happiness vs Income

Country-year observations (WHR). Line shows a fitted linear trend.

Finding: Income is strongly associated with happiness; returns typically diminish at higher income levels.

3.2 Happiness vs Vacation/Leisure (Work–Life Balance Proxy)

OECD leisure minutes/day merged at the country level.

Finding: Leisure time shows a positive association with happiness, consistent with the idea that time off and work–life balance can matter for well-being—especially after basic needs are met.

3.3 Happiness vs Corruption Perceptions

Higher values reflect stronger (more favorable) corruption-related contribution in the WHR explained components.

Finding: Corruption perceptions are strongly related to happiness. Countries with stronger institutional trust and lower perceived corruption tend to report higher well-being.

6. Key Takeaways

  • Income remains a strong correlate of happiness, especially where incomes are lower.
  • Vacation/leisure (time-off proxy) is positively related to happiness, suggesting work–life balance matters for well-being.
  • Governance/institutional trust (corruption perceptions) is also strongly associated with happiness and can help explain cross-country differences beyond income alone.

7. Limitations & Next Steps

  • Correlation does not imply causation; omitted variables may drive patterns.
  • Country averages mask inequality within countries.
  • Future work can estimate income “tipping points” where additional income yields smaller happiness gains than additional time off.
